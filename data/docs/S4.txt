TITLE: Placebo response in respiratory trials (mock)
YEAR: 2021
TEXT:
The placebo effect in asthma and COPD clinical trials can significantly impact the evaluation of novel therapeutics.
Studies show that subjective endpoints, such as patient-reported symptom scores, are highly susceptible to placebo responses.
Conversely, objective physiological measures like Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second (FEV1) are less affected.
Strategies to mitigate placebo response include centralized rater training and the use of objective biomarkers.
Run-in periods are frequently employed to establish baseline stability and exclude high placebo responders prior to randomization.
Understanding the magnitude of the placebo effect is crucial for accurate sample size calculation and statistical powering.
The mode of administration (e.g., inhaler vs. subcutaneous injection) can influence the strength of the placebo effect.
Double-blind, placebo-controlled designs remain the gold standard, despite the challenges posed by high placebo response rates.
Researchers must carefully design patient interaction protocols to avoid inadvertently inflating expectation biases.
Post-hoc analyses often aim to identify demographic or disease characteristics associated with higher placebo responses.
